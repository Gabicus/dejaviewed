Eight Eras of Data

Each era multiplied the scope — and the complexity. ↓

Section overview. Walk down through the 8 eras. The complexity bar grows each era to show the exponential scope increase.

ERA 1 · 2024

Original Dataset

~200entries · installation-level, dirty data
ERA 1 (2024). Starting point: ~200 messy installation-level entries from old Gas Turbine World magazines — very "dirty," no standardized methodology, duplicate entries, unclear sourcing.

ERA 2 · 2024

Initial Analysis & EIA Crossing

700+units · matched to EIA-860
ERA 2 (2024). Manually checked installations against EIA-860 one by one, expanded installations into individual units (700+), and custom-defined a unique unit identifier.

ERA 3 · 2024

Tableau Logic Development

Tableau Preprecursive mass-data logic
ERA 3 (2024). The turning point: built mass-data logic in Tableau Prep to future-proof later updates. Recursive — the output spreadsheet cycles back into the process. This is what made later scaling possible.

ERA 4 · October 2024

Initial Dashboard Construction

Dashboard Livepublished to NETL · Oct 2024
ERA 4 (Oct 2024). First public dashboard on the NETL website — congregated confirmed operating units into plant locations and plotted them on a map. Advanced sorting and selection options to analyze by plant, OEM, region, model, firing class, etc.

ERA 5 · Early 2025

First Major Data Expansion

2,600+entries · installed 1999–2024 · EIA-923
ERA 5 (early 2025). Pushed the start year back from 2010 to 1999, added 2024 data from EIA. Dataset grew from ~780 to over 2,600 entries.

ERA 6 · August 2025

First Major Dashboard Update

Capacity Factorsutilization analysis · EIA-923
ERA 6 (Aug 2025). Greatly improved data-import logic, incorporated EIA-923 to calculate unit capacity factors, and improved emissions factors.

ERA 7 · February 2026

Second Major Dashboard Update

Historical Analysisgeneration 2014–2024 · EIA-923
ERA 7 (Feb 2026). Brought in past EIA-923 reports (2014 forward) for historical utilization data, overhauled the dashboard presentation, added treemap charts per tab, and added a Historical tab to compare years directly.

ERA 8 · 2026 · Current

Current Efforts

4,000+entries · 1960–present · hourly data
ERA 8 (2026, current). Expanding to all available EIA-860 data back to 1960 — over 4,000 total entries. Will incorporate 2025 data once released. Generation 2015–2025, EIA-to-EPA crosswalk, hourly data. From ~200 dirty rows to 4,000+ entries with hourly generator data — exponential scope growth. The 2026 jump — daily to hourly — is roughly two orders of magnitude more data.

How do you scale a project like this?

2 MB
1 yr × monthly × generator
7 GB
10 yr × daily × generator
100 GB
10 yr × hourly × generator

One person. 2 MB → 100 GB. You bring in a co-pilot.

The pivot. Each step up in temporal resolution or time range multiplies the data volume — and the complexity of cleaning, validating, and visualizing it. 1 yr × monthly × generator ≈ 2 MB; 10 yr × daily ≈ 7 GB; 10 yr × hourly ≈ 100 GB. The scope outgrew hand-processing — the answer was AI, specifically DOE's own platform, Joulix.

How I Use Joulix

"AI" as a
User Interface

Rule #1 — understand it's just a machine doing machine things.

My mental model: AI isn't a genie, it's a new kind of user interface. First principle — it's a machine doing machine things at a scale we've never had.

What's Actually Happening

It is not thinking. It is calculating.

Pattern recognition at a scale orders of magnitude beyond anything before — predicting the next token from billions of examples.

Once you treat it like a machine, you prompt it like one — and it gets dramatically more useful.

Demystify it. No magic — pattern recognition / next-token prediction at massive scale. Treating it as a calculating machine (not a colleague) is what makes prompting click.

The Trick

Talk to it like a robot. Be explicit. Iterate.

You: Free-write what you want. Then free-write how you think you'd do it.

It: Fills in the engineering — the syntax, the edge cases, the boilerplate.

You provide the vision. The AI fills in the execution.

The whole technique in one slide: state intent plainly, sketch your approach, let the model handle syntax. Then iterate. You keep the judgment; it does the typing.

How I Use Joulix · talk to it like a robot

I free-write what I want, then how I'd do it

My prompt

I'd like you to a write python script to go through this hourly data file, emissions-hourly-2024-q1_100k.csv. This is just an export of 100k rows as the full data set is about 7 gigs. I only want to look at IDs that match another data set I have NETLTurbineID.xlsx. In my hourly data, a new Column "UniquID" will be added based on [Facility ID] + " – " + [Unit ID] in the hourly data set. My resulting data will only keep Unique IDs matched between NETLTurbineID.xlsx and emissions-hourly-2024-q1_100k.csv. I'd like to begin by loading this data into a data frame for analysis, visualization and data transformation exports. This is where we'll start.

Joulix outputs python I can run · VS Code

Talk to it like a robot. I free-write what I want, then how I think I'd accomplish it. The full prompt describes the file, the row limit (100k of ~7 GB), the join to NETLTurbineID.xlsx, the UniqueID construction, and the goal of loading into a dataframe. Joulix returns runnable python I open in VS Code.

How I Use Joulix · be explicit

Now I want a GUI tool — so I ask for one

My prompt

I want to focus on your thoughts toward creating this GUI analysis tool first. I want to add two core functions to this:

The first output I need is based on a calculations at the day level of the number of starts and stops of a generator unit each day. This is inferred by counting the number of times in a day [Date] in the order of [Hour] starting at 0 and ending at 23, a generator [UniqueID] has a value in [Gross Load (MW)], and then when that value goes to 0, in a day.

The resulting Table or data frame would be aggregated at the day for each UniqueID with it's "Total Starts" and "Total Stops" in a day, for all days in the data set. And I'd like the ability to export that table.

It built an interactive Streamlit Dashboard

Be explicit. I spell out exactly what I want: daily starts/stops per UniqueID, inferred by counting zero→non-zero transitions in [Gross Load (MW)] across hours 0–23, aggregated per day, with export. It didn't just write a script — it built a full Streamlit GUI.

When the Floor Disappears

Marcy Pedestrian Bridge collapse
Marcy, New York · October 2002
Curved steel tub girder buckled during concrete deck placement — 1 ironworker killed, 9 injured.

No top-flange bracing on the steel tub girder. Lateral torsional buckling under construction loads.

The software modeled the finished composite section perfectly.
Nobody checked the construction sequence.

The tool was right about the wrong question.

A real failure mode: the software got the final design right, but no one with the foundational knowledge checked the construction-phase loads. The tool answered the wrong question correctly.
